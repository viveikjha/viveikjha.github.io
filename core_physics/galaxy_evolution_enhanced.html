Introduction to Galaxy Evolution

Galaxy evolution is the study of how galaxies form and change over cosmic time. It seeks to understand the physical processes that shape the observed properties of galaxies, including their morphologies, stellar populations, gas content, chemical composition, and scaling relations. As the fundamental building blocks of the large-scale structure of the universe, galaxies provide crucial insights into cosmology, star formation, and the interplay between baryonic matter and dark matter.

The historical development of galaxy evolution studies began with the classification of galaxy morphologies by Edwin Hubble in the 1920s and 1930s. Early models focused on monolithic collapse scenarios, where galaxies formed rapidly in the early universe. However, the discovery of the cosmic microwave background and the development of the Cold Dark Matter (CDM) cosmological model in the 1980s led to the modern paradigm of hierarchical structure formation, where galaxies grow through the gradual accretion of smaller structures and gas.

This module follows the comprehensive approach of Mo, van den Bosch, and White, complemented by the observational perspective of Conselice and the theoretical insights of Somerville and Davé, to provide a thorough understanding of galaxy evolution. We will explore the key physical processes involved, including dark matter halo formation, gas accretion and cooling, star formation, feedback, mergers, and environmental effects, emphasizing both theoretical models and observational constraints across cosmic time.

Cosmological Framework and Dark Matter Halos

Galaxy evolution takes place within the framework of the standard ΛCDM cosmological model, which describes a universe dominated by dark energy (Λ) and cold dark matter (CDM).

Hierarchical Structure Formation

In the ΛCDM model, structure forms hierarchically: small density fluctuations in the early universe grow through gravitational instability, forming small dark matter halos first. These halos then merge over time to form larger halos, creating a cosmic web of filaments, sheets, and voids. Galaxies are thought to form and reside within these dark matter halos.

The growth of dark matter halos can be described by analytical models like the Press-Schechter formalism and its extensions, or simulated numerically using N-body simulations. These simulations show that dark matter halos have characteristic density profiles, often described by the Navarro-Frenk-White (NFW) profile:

\rho(r) = \frac{\rho_s}{(r/r_s)(1+r/r_s)^2}

NFW density profile, where ρs is a characteristic density and rs is a scale radius

The mass function of dark matter halos—the number density of halos as a function of mass—is a key prediction of the ΛCDM model and provides a fundamental link between cosmology and galaxy formation.

Halo Assembly History

The assembly history of a dark matter halo—the sequence of mergers and accretion events that built it up—plays a crucial role in determining the properties of the galaxy it hosts. Halos that assemble earlier tend to host older, redder galaxies, while halos that assemble later tend to host younger, bluer galaxies. This concept is known as "assembly bias."

Merger trees, extracted from N-body simulations or generated using semi-analytical methods, provide a statistical description of halo assembly histories and are a key input for models of galaxy formation.

Problem 1: Halo Mass Function

The Press-Schechter mass function gives the comoving number density of dark matter halos with mass M at redshift z. Qualitatively describe how the halo mass function changes with redshift. At a fixed mass (e.g., 1012 M☉), are there more halos at high redshift or low redshift? At a fixed redshift (e.g., z = 0), are there more low-mass halos or high-mass halos?

Solution:

1. The Press-Schechter mass function describes the abundance of dark matter halos as a function of mass and redshift.

2. Change with redshift: Due to hierarchical structure formation, halos grow over time through mergers. Therefore, at higher redshifts, massive halos are rarer, and the mass function is steeper (more low-mass halos relative to high-mass halos). At lower redshifts, halos have had more time to merge and grow, so massive halos become more common, and the mass function flattens at the high-mass end.

3. Abundance at fixed mass: For a fixed mass like 1012 M☉ (typical mass of a Milky Way-like halo), these halos are relatively common at low redshift (z = 0) but become increasingly rare at higher redshifts because they haven't had enough time to assemble. Thus, there are more 1012 M☉ halos at low redshift than at high redshift.

4. Abundance at fixed redshift: At any given redshift, the mass function is always a steeply declining function of mass. This means there are always far more low-mass halos than high-mass halos. For example, at z = 0, there are vastly more dwarf galaxy-sized halos (109-1010 M☉) than cluster-sized halos (1014-1015 M☉).

Baryonic Processes: Gas Accretion and Cooling

While dark matter dominates the gravitational dynamics, the visible components of galaxies are made of baryonic matter (protons, neutrons, electrons). The evolution of this baryonic component, primarily gas, is crucial for understanding galaxy formation.

Gas Accretion

Galaxies acquire gas from the surrounding intergalactic medium (IGM) through several mechanisms:

  • Hot mode accretion: In massive halos (Mhalo > 1012 M☉), gas falling into the halo is shock-heated to the virial temperature (Tvir ~ 106-107 K) and forms a hot, quasi-static halo. Gas then cools radiatively from this hot halo and accretes onto the central galaxy.
  • Cold mode accretion: In low-mass halos (Mhalo < 1012 M☉) or along dense filaments feeding massive halos, gas can radiate its energy efficiently and flow directly onto the central galaxy without being shock-heated to the virial temperature. This is thought to be the dominant mode of gas accretion at high redshifts.
  • Mergers: Gas can be brought into galaxies through mergers with other gas-rich galaxies.

Gas Cooling

For gas to form stars, it must cool and lose pressure support. The primary cooling mechanisms depend on temperature:

  • T > 107 K: Bremsstrahlung (free-free emission) dominates.
  • 104 K < T < 107 K: Line emission from collisionally excited ions of heavy elements (metals) dominates. Cooling is highly sensitive to metallicity.
  • T < 104 K: Line emission from neutral atoms and molecules (e.g., H₂, CO) and cooling by dust grains become important.

The cooling time tcool is the time it takes for gas to radiate away its thermal energy. It depends on the gas density, temperature, and metallicity. Comparing the cooling time to the halo dynamical time tdyn determines whether gas can cool efficiently:

t_{cool} = \frac{3/2 n k_B T}{n_e n_H \Lambda(T, Z)}

Cooling time, where n is the total number density, T is temperature, ne and nH are electron and hydrogen densities, and Λ(T, Z) is the cooling function

If tcool < tdyn, gas can cool rapidly and collapse to form stars. If tcool > tdyn, cooling is inefficient, and gas remains hot and pressure-supported.

Angular Momentum

Gas accreting onto halos typically possesses angular momentum acquired from tidal torques during the large-scale structure formation process. As the gas cools and collapses, it conserves angular momentum, leading to the formation of rotationally supported disks, which are characteristic features of spiral galaxies.

The specific angular momentum j of the gas is related to the halo spin parameter λ:

\lambda = \frac{J |E|^{1/2}}{G M^{5/2}}

Halo spin parameter, where J is the total angular momentum, E is the total energy, and M is the halo mass

Simulations show that λ typically has a value around 0.04, leading to disk sizes that are roughly consistent with observations, although the detailed distribution of angular momentum remains a challenge for models.

Problem 2: Virial Temperature

Estimate the virial temperature Tvir for a dark matter halo with mass M = 1012 M☉ collapsing at redshift z = 0. Assume the halo has virialized, meaning its kinetic energy is related to its potential energy by the virial theorem.

Solution:

1. The virial temperature is defined as the temperature at which the thermal energy per particle equals the gravitational potential energy per particle:

(3/2) kB Tvir ≈ G M mp / Rvir

where kB is Boltzmann's constant, M is the halo mass, mp is the proton mass, and Rvir is the virial radius.

2. The virial radius is the radius within which the mean density is Δvir times the critical density of the universe ρcrit(z). For a flat ΛCDM universe at z = 0, Δvir ≈ 200.

M = (4/3)π Rvir³ × Δvir × ρcrit(0)

3. The critical density at z = 0 is:

ρcrit(0) = 3 H₀² / (8πG) ≈ 9.2 × 10⁻³⁰ g/cm³ (for H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc)

4. Solve for Rvir:

Rvir = [3M / (4π Δvir ρcrit(0))]1/3

Rvir = [3 × 1012 × 1.99 × 1033 g / (4π × 200 × 9.2 × 10⁻³⁰ g/cm³)]1/3

Rvir = [5.97 × 1045 / 2.31 × 10⁻²⁶]1/3 cm

Rvir = (2.58 × 1071)1/3 cm = 6.37 × 1023 cm ≈ 206 kpc

5. Now calculate the virial temperature:

Tvir ≈ (2/3) × (G M mp) / (kB Rvir)

Tvir ≈ (2/3) × (6.67 × 10⁻⁸ × 1012 × 1.99 × 1033 × 1.67 × 10⁻²⁴) / (1.38 × 10⁻¹⁶ × 6.37 × 1023) K

Tvir ≈ (2/3) × (2.22 × 1035) / (8.79 × 107) K

Tvir ≈ 1.68 × 1027 K

6. Wait, there must be a mistake in the calculation. Let's re-evaluate the virial temperature formula. It's often expressed in terms of the circular velocity Vc = √(GM/R):

kB Tvir ≈ μ mp Vc²

where μ is the mean molecular weight (≈ 0.6 for ionized primordial gas).

7. Calculate Vc:

Vc² = G M / Rvir = 6.67 × 10⁻⁸ × 1012 × 1.99 × 1033 / 6.37 × 1023 (cm/s)²

Vc² = 2.08 × 1014 (cm/s)²

Vc = 1.44 × 107 cm/s = 144 km/s

8. Calculate Tvir:

Tvir ≈ (μ mp Vc²) / kB

Tvir ≈ (0.6 × 1.67 × 10⁻²⁴ g × 2.08 × 1014 (cm/s)²) / (1.38 × 10⁻¹⁶ erg/K)

Tvir ≈ 2.09 × 10⁻¹⁰ erg / 1.38 × 10⁻¹⁶ erg/K

Tvir ≈ 1.5 × 106 K

9. This result (Tvir ≈ 1.5 million K) is much more reasonable. Halos with masses around 1012 M☉ have virial temperatures of ~106 K, which marks the transition between efficient cooling (cold mode accretion) and inefficient cooling (hot mode accretion).

Star Formation and Chemical Evolution

Star formation is the process by which cold, dense gas clouds collapse under gravity to form stars. It is a key process in galaxy evolution, converting gas into stars and enriching the interstellar medium (ISM) with heavy elements.

Conditions for Star Formation

Star formation occurs primarily in giant molecular clouds (GMCs), which are cold (T ~ 10-20 K) and dense (n > 100 cm⁻³) regions of the ISM. The conditions for gravitational collapse are determined by the Jeans criterion:

M_J = \left(\frac{5 k_B T}{G \mu m_p}\right)^{3/2} \left(\frac{3}{4\pi\rho}\right)^{1/2}

Jeans mass, the minimum mass required for a cloud to collapse under its own gravity

Regions within GMCs that exceed the Jeans mass can collapse to form stars. However, star formation is generally inefficient, with only a few percent of the gas in a GMC typically converted into stars per dynamical time. This inefficiency is attributed to factors like turbulence, magnetic fields, and feedback from young stars.

Star Formation Rate (SFR)

The star formation rate (SFR) is the rate at which gas is converted into stars in a galaxy, typically measured in M☉/yr. Empirical relations connect the SFR to the properties of the gas reservoir:

  • Kennicutt-Schmidt law: Relates the surface density of star formation (ΣSFR) to the surface density of gas (Σgas):

    \Sigma_{SFR} \propto \Sigma_{gas}^N

    Kennicutt-Schmidt law, with N ≈ 1.4

  • Molecular gas relation: The SFR correlates more tightly with the surface density of molecular gas (ΣH₂) than with the total gas density.

The cosmic star formation history—the evolution of the average SFR density of the universe over time—shows a peak at z ~ 2-3 and declines toward the present day.

Initial Mass Function (IMF)

The initial mass function (IMF) describes the distribution of stellar masses at birth. It is typically parameterized as a power law or a series of power laws:

\xi(M) = dN/dM \propto M^{-\alpha}

IMF power law, where ξ(M) is the number of stars per unit mass interval

Commonly used IMFs include the Salpeter IMF (α = 2.35 for M > 0.5 M☉), the Kroupa IMF, and the Chabrier IMF. The IMF is remarkably universal, showing little variation across different environments in the local universe, although its form in the early universe remains uncertain.

Chemical Evolution

Stars synthesize heavy elements (metals) through nuclear fusion and release them into the ISM through stellar winds and supernova explosions. This process, known as chemical enrichment, gradually increases the metallicity of gas and subsequent generations of stars over cosmic time.

Key aspects of chemical evolution include:

  • Yields: The amount of different elements produced and ejected by stars of different masses and types of supernovae (Type Ia and core-collapse).
  • Gas flows: Inflows of metal-poor gas from the IGM dilute the metallicity, while outflows of enriched gas (driven by feedback) remove metals from the galaxy.
  • Mass-metallicity relation: More massive galaxies tend to have higher metallicities, likely due to their deeper gravitational potential wells, which retain enriched gas more effectively.

Simple closed-box models provide a basic framework for understanding chemical evolution, but realistic models must account for gas flows and the detailed yields from different stellar populations.

Problem 3: Kennicutt-Schmidt Law

A spiral galaxy disk has an average gas surface density of Σgas = 10 M☉/pc². Using the Kennicutt-Schmidt law ΣSFR = 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ (Σgas / (M☉/pc²))1.4 M☉/yr/kpc², estimate the average star formation rate surface density in the disk.

Solution:

1. The Kennicutt-Schmidt law relates the star formation rate surface density to the gas surface density:

ΣSFR = A × (Σgas)N

where A = 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ M☉/yr/kpc² / (M☉/pc²)1.4 and N = 1.4.

2. Substitute the given gas surface density Σgas = 10 M☉/pc²:

ΣSFR = 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ × (10)1.4 M☉/yr/kpc²

3. Calculate 101.4:

101.4 = 101 × 100.4 ≈ 10 × 2.51 = 25.1

4. Calculate ΣSFR:

ΣSFR = 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ × 25.1 M☉/yr/kpc²

ΣSFR = 6.28 × 10⁻³ M☉/yr/kpc²

5. Therefore, the estimated average star formation rate surface density in the disk is about 6.3 × 10⁻³ M☉/yr/kpc².

Feedback Processes

Feedback refers to the processes by which energy and momentum injected by stars and active galactic nuclei (AGN) affect the surrounding gas, regulating star formation and galaxy growth.

Stellar Feedback

Feedback from massive stars (M > 8 M☉) plays a crucial role in shaping the ISM and regulating star formation on galactic scales. Key mechanisms include:

  • Photoionization: Ultraviolet radiation from young, massive stars ionizes the surrounding gas, creating HII regions and heating the gas to ~104 K.
  • Stellar winds: Massive stars lose mass through powerful winds, injecting energy and momentum into the ISM.
  • Supernovae: Core-collapse supernovae at the end of massive stars' lives inject enormous amounts of energy (~1051 erg) and heavy elements into the ISM, driving shocks and creating hot bubbles.

Collectively, these processes can drive galactic winds—large-scale outflows of gas from galaxies—which remove gas and metals from the galaxy, suppressing subsequent star formation and enriching the circumgalactic medium (CGM) and IGM.

AGN Feedback

Feedback from AGN, powered by accretion onto supermassive black holes, is thought to be particularly important in regulating the growth of massive galaxies. Key mechanisms include:

  • Radiative (quasar) mode: Radiation pressure and winds from luminous AGN (L/LEdd > 0.01) can expel gas from the host galaxy.
  • Kinetic (radio) mode: Jets from radio-loud AGN (typically in low-accretion-rate systems) can inflate cavities in the surrounding hot gas (e.g., in galaxy clusters), preventing gas cooling and accretion.

AGN feedback is invoked to explain several key observations:

  • The tight correlation between black hole mass and host galaxy bulge properties (M-σ relation).
  • The quenching of star formation in massive galaxies, leading to the observed red sequence.
  • The prevention of catastrophic cooling in galaxy clusters (the cooling flow problem).

Impact on Galaxy Properties

Feedback processes have profound effects on galaxy properties:

  • Regulation of star formation: Feedback limits the efficiency of star formation, preventing galaxies from converting all their gas into stars rapidly.
  • Galactic winds: Outflows driven by feedback remove gas and metals, shaping the mass-metallicity relation and enriching the CGM/IGM.
  • Morphology: Feedback can influence the structure of galaxies, potentially contributing to the formation of bulges or disrupting disks.
  • Galaxy bimodality: The combination of stellar and AGN feedback is thought to be responsible for the observed bimodality of galaxy properties, separating blue, star-forming galaxies from red, quiescent galaxies.

Problem 4: Supernova Energy Injection

A galaxy forms stars at a rate of 10 M☉/yr. Assuming a standard IMF (e.g., Kroupa), approximately one core-collapse supernova occurs for every 100 M☉ of stars formed. If each supernova injects 1051 erg of energy, calculate the total energy injection rate from supernovae in this galaxy. If only 10% of this energy effectively couples to the ISM to drive winds, what is the power available for feedback?

Solution:

1. The star formation rate is SFR = 10 M☉/yr.

2. The rate of core-collapse supernovae (SN) is:

RateSN = SFR / (Mass per SN)

RateSN = (10 M☉/yr) / (100 M☉/SN) = 0.1 SN/yr

3. The energy injected per supernova is ESN = 1051 erg.

4. The total energy injection rate from supernovae is:

PSN = RateSN × ESN

PSN = (0.1 SN/yr) × (1051 erg/SN)

PSN = 1050 erg/yr

5. Convert the power to erg/s:

PSN = 1050 erg/yr / (3.15 × 107 s/yr) ≈ 3.17 × 1042 erg/s

6. If the coupling efficiency is 10%, the power available for feedback is:

Pfeedback = 0.1 × PSN

Pfeedback = 0.1 × 3.17 × 1042 erg/s = 3.17 × 1041 erg/s

7. This feedback power (≈ 3 × 1041 erg/s) is substantial and can significantly impact the ISM, potentially driving galactic winds, especially in lower-mass galaxies with shallower potential wells.

Galaxy Mergers and Environmental Effects

Galaxies do not evolve in isolation. Interactions and mergers with other galaxies, as well as the influence of the large-scale environment, play crucial roles in shaping their properties.

Galaxy Mergers

Galaxy mergers are collisions between two or more galaxies. They are a fundamental aspect of hierarchical structure formation and can dramatically alter galaxy properties:

  • Major mergers: Mergers between galaxies of comparable mass (mass ratio > 1:4). These are thought to be responsible for:
    • Transforming spiral galaxies into elliptical galaxies by destroying disks and building bulges.
    • Triggering intense bursts of star formation (starbursts) by compressing gas.
    • Fueling AGN activity by driving gas toward the central black hole.
    • Growing the masses of supermassive black holes through mergers.
  • Minor mergers: Mergers between galaxies with large mass ratios (mass ratio < 1:4). These are more common than major mergers and can:
    • Thicken existing disks.
    • Add stars and gas to the halo.
    • Contribute to bulge growth over time.
    • Trigger moderate levels of star formation or AGN activity.

The frequency and importance of mergers evolve with cosmic time, being more common at higher redshifts when the universe was denser.

Secular Evolution

Secular evolution refers to the slow, gradual changes in galaxy structure driven by internal processes, such as:

  • Bars: Non-axisymmetric structures in disk galaxies that can redistribute angular momentum, drive gas inflows, fuel central star formation (pseudo-bulges), and potentially trigger AGN activity.
  • Spiral arms: Density waves that can trigger star formation and influence the structure of disks.
  • Disk instabilities: Gravitational instabilities that can lead to clump formation and migration, particularly in gas-rich disks at high redshift.

Secular processes are thought to be important in shaping the properties of disk galaxies, particularly at lower redshifts when major mergers are less frequent.

Environmental Effects

The evolution of a galaxy can be significantly influenced by its environment, particularly in dense regions like galaxy groups and clusters:

  • Ram pressure stripping: As a galaxy moves through the hot, dense intracluster medium (ICM), the ram pressure can strip gas from the galaxy's disk, quenching star formation.
  • Strangulation (starvation): The removal of the galaxy's hot gas halo by the cluster environment prevents further gas accretion, gradually shutting off star formation as the existing gas reservoir is consumed.
  • Tidal interactions: Gravitational interactions with the cluster potential or nearby galaxies can distort the galaxy's structure and trigger star formation or AGN activity (harassment).
  • Mergers: The high density of galaxies in clusters increases the likelihood of mergers, although the high relative velocities can make mergers less efficient than in the field.

These environmental processes contribute to the observed morphology-density relation, where elliptical and S0 galaxies are more common in dense environments, while spiral galaxies dominate in the field.

Problem 5: Ram Pressure Stripping

A spiral galaxy with a gas surface density Σgas = 10 M☉/pc² is moving at v = 1000 km/s through an intracluster medium (ICM) with density ρICM = 10⁻²⁷ g/cm³. Estimate the ram pressure Pram exerted on the galaxy. Compare this to the gravitational restoring force per unit area Fgrav/A ≈ 2πG Σ* Σgas, assuming a stellar surface density Σ* = 100 M☉/pc². Will the gas be stripped?

Solution:

1. The ram pressure is given by:

Pram = ρICM v²

2. Substitute the values:

Pram = (10⁻²⁷ g/cm³) × (1000 km/s)²

Pram = 10⁻²⁷ g/cm³ × (10⁸ cm/s)²

Pram = 10⁻²⁷ × 10¹⁶ dyne/cm² = 10⁻¹¹ dyne/cm²

3. The gravitational restoring force per unit area is approximately:

Fgrav/A ≈ 2πG Σ* Σgas

4. Convert surface densities to g/cm²:

Σgas = 10 M☉/pc² = 10 × 1.99 × 1033 g / (3.086 × 1018 cm)² = 2.09 × 10⁻³ g/cm²

Σ* = 100 M☉/pc² = 2.09 × 10⁻² g/cm²

5. Calculate the restoring force per unit area:

Fgrav/A ≈ 2π × (6.67 × 10⁻⁸ cm³ g⁻¹ s⁻²) × (2.09 × 10⁻² g/cm²) × (2.09 × 10⁻³ g/cm²)

Fgrav/A ≈ 1.83 × 10⁻¹¹ dyne/cm²

6. Compare ram pressure and restoring force:

Pram = 10⁻¹¹ dyne/cm²

Fgrav/A ≈ 1.83 × 10⁻¹¹ dyne/cm²

7. In this case, Pram is comparable to, but slightly less than, the gravitational restoring force per unit area at this gas surface density. Gas stripping occurs when Pram > Fgrav/A. Therefore, gas at this surface density might be partially stripped or significantly disturbed, but perhaps not completely removed. Gas at lower surface densities (e.g., in the outer disk) would be more easily stripped.

Observational Probes and Techniques

Studying galaxy evolution relies on observations across cosmic time, using various techniques to probe different aspects of galaxy properties.

Deep Surveys

Deep surveys use long exposures with powerful telescopes to detect faint, distant galaxies, providing snapshots of the galaxy population at different epochs. Key surveys include:

  • Hubble Deep Fields (HDF, HUDF, CANDELS): Deep optical and near-infrared imaging with the Hubble Space Telescope, revealing galaxies out to z ~ 10.
  • Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS): Large-area optical imaging and spectroscopy of millions of galaxies in the local universe.
  • Subaru Deep Fields, VLT Deep Fields: Deep imaging from ground-based telescopes.
  • ALMA and VLA deep fields: Probing gas content and star formation in distant galaxies at submillimeter and radio wavelengths.

Measuring Galaxy Properties

Various techniques are used to measure key galaxy properties:

  • Redshifts: Measured from spectroscopy (spectroscopic redshift) or multi-band photometry (photometric redshift), providing distance estimates.
  • Stellar masses: Estimated by fitting stellar population synthesis (SPS) models to observed photometry or spectra.
  • Star formation rates: Measured using various indicators calibrated to the UV continuum (from young, massive stars), recombination lines (e.g., Hα), or infrared emission (from dust heated by young stars).
  • Morphologies: Classified visually or using quantitative measures like concentration, asymmetry, and smoothness (CAS parameters) or Sérsic profiles.
  • Gas content: Measured from emission lines of neutral hydrogen (HI 21cm line) or molecular gas tracers (e.g., CO rotational lines).
  • Metallicities: Estimated from emission line ratios in HII regions or absorption lines in stellar spectra.

Connecting Galaxies Across Time

Understanding galaxy evolution requires connecting galaxy populations observed at different redshifts. Methods include:

  • Evolution of number densities: Tracking the abundance of galaxies with specific properties (e.g., mass, SFR) over time.
  • Progenitor bias: Accounting for the fact that galaxies observed at high redshift are not necessarily the direct progenitors of galaxies with similar properties observed today.
  • Semi-analytical models and simulations: Using theoretical models constrained by observations to track the evolution of individual galaxies and populations over time.
  • Archaeological approach: Studying the resolved stellar populations in nearby galaxies to reconstruct their past star formation and assembly histories.

Problem 6: Stellar Mass Estimation

A galaxy at redshift z = 1 has an observed K-band magnitude of K = 21. Assuming a mass-to-light ratio in the K-band of (M/L)K = 0.5 M☉/L☉,K (typical for a moderately old stellar population), estimate the stellar mass of the galaxy. The absolute magnitude of the Sun in the K-band is MK,☉ = 3.28.

Solution:

1. First, calculate the absolute K-band magnitude MK of the galaxy using the distance modulus formula:

MK = mK - 5 log₁₀(dL/10 pc)

where mK = 21 is the apparent magnitude and dL is the luminosity distance.

2. The luminosity distance to z = 1 in a standard ΛCDM cosmology is approximately dL ≈ 6600 Mpc ≈ 2.15 × 1028 cm ≈ 6.97 × 109 pc.

3. Calculate the distance modulus:

DM = 5 log₁₀(6.97 × 109 / 10) = 5 log₁₀(6.97 × 108)

DM = 5 × (log₁₀(6.97) + 8) = 5 × (0.843 + 8) = 5 × 8.843 = 44.22

4. Calculate the absolute magnitude:

MK = 21 - 44.22 = -23.22

5. The luminosity LK relative to the Sun's K-band luminosity L☉,K is related to the absolute magnitudes by:

MK - MK,☉ = -2.5 log₁₀(LK / L☉,K)

6. Solve for the luminosity ratio:

log₁₀(LK / L☉,K) = (MK,☉ - MK) / 2.5

log₁₀(LK / L☉,K) = (3.28 - (-23.22)) / 2.5 = 26.5 / 2.5 = 10.6

LK / L☉,K = 1010.6 ≈ 3.98 × 1010

7. The stellar mass M* is given by:

M* = (M/L)K × LK

M* = (0.5 M☉/L☉,K) × (3.98 × 1010 L☉,K)

M* = 1.99 × 1010 M☉

8. Therefore, the estimated stellar mass of the galaxy is approximately 2 × 1010 M☉. Note that this estimate is sensitive to the assumed mass-to-light ratio, which depends on the age and metallicity of the stellar population, and requires careful modeling in practice.

Current Frontiers and Open Questions

Galaxy evolution remains a vibrant field of research with many fundamental questions still unanswered.

The First Galaxies and Reionization

Understanding the formation and properties of the first galaxies during the epoch of reionization (z > 6) is a major frontier:

  • When and how did the first stars and galaxies form?
  • What were the properties of these first galaxies (masses, sizes, SFRs, metallicities)?
  • What sources were responsible for reionizing the universe?
  • How did the first supermassive black holes form and grow?

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is poised to revolutionize this field by directly observing these early galaxies.

The Baryon Cycle

Understanding the flow of baryons (gas) into, through, and out of galaxies—the baryon cycle—is crucial:

  • How do galaxies accrete gas from the cosmic web, and what are the relative roles of hot and cold mode accretion?
  • What are the physical mechanisms driving galactic winds, and how do their properties depend on galaxy mass and redshift?
  • What is the structure and composition of the circumgalactic medium (CGM), the interface between galaxies and the IGM?
  • How efficiently is gas recycled back into galaxies after being ejected in winds?

The Regulation of Star Formation

Despite decades of research, the processes that regulate star formation efficiency remain debated:

  • What sets the slope and normalization of the Kennicutt-Schmidt law?
  • What is the relative importance of different feedback mechanisms (supernovae, radiation pressure, AGN) in regulating star formation?
  • How does star formation efficiency vary with galaxy properties and environment?
  • What processes quench star formation in massive galaxies?

The Nature of Dark Matter

While the ΛCDM model is successful on large scales, challenges remain on small scales, potentially hinting at new dark matter physics:

  • Cusp-core problem: Simulations predict cuspy central density profiles for dark matter halos, while observations often suggest cored profiles in dwarf galaxies.
  • Missing satellites problem: Simulations predict many more small subhalos around Milky Way-like galaxies than are observed.
  • Too-big-to-fail problem: The most massive subhalos predicted by simulations seem inconsistent with the properties of observed dwarf spheroidal galaxies.

These challenges may be resolved by baryonic physics (feedback) or could indicate deviations from the standard CDM model (e.g., warm or self-interacting dark matter).

Future Observational Facilities

Upcoming facilities will provide transformative data for galaxy evolution studies:

  • JWST: Probing the first galaxies and reionization.
  • Roman Space Telescope: Large-area near-infrared surveys to study galaxy evolution over wide fields.
  • Euclid: Mapping the large-scale structure and weak lensing to constrain cosmology and galaxy clustering.
  • Vera C. Rubin Observatory (LSST): Deep, wide-field optical survey to study galaxy evolution, transients, and dark energy.
  • SKA and ngVLA: Next-generation radio arrays to map neutral hydrogen and star formation across cosmic time.
  • 30-meter class telescopes (ELT, GMT, TMT): Providing unprecedented sensitivity and resolution for detailed studies of distant galaxies.
