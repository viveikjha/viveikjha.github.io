Introduction to Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics represents one of the most profound shifts in scientific understanding, fundamentally altering our perception of reality at the atomic and subatomic levels. Developed in the early 20th century, it emerged from the failure of classical physics to explain phenomena like blackbody radiation, the photoelectric effect, and atomic spectra. Quantum mechanics introduces concepts such as wave-particle duality, quantization of energy and momentum, the uncertainty principle, and entanglement, which have no analogues in classical physics.

The historical development involved pivotal contributions from Planck (quantization), Einstein (photoelectric effect), Bohr (atomic model), de Broglie (matter waves), Heisenberg (matrix mechanics, uncertainty principle), Schrödinger (wave mechanics), Dirac (relativistic quantum mechanics), and many others. This collective effort led to a mathematical formalism that, despite its counterintuitive nature, has been experimentally verified to extraordinary precision and forms the basis for understanding chemistry, condensed matter physics, nuclear physics, particle physics, and technologies like lasers, transistors, and quantum computing.

This module introduces the core principles and mathematical formalism of non-relativistic quantum mechanics, following the pedagogical clarity of Griffiths' Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, complemented by Zettili’s comprehensive problem-based perspective, to provide a robust foundation for understanding the quantum world.

Foundations: Wave-Particle Duality and the Schrödinger Equation

The departure from classical physics began with the realization that energy and light could behave as discrete packets (quanta) and that particles like electrons could exhibit wave-like properties.

Wave-Particle Duality

Louis de Broglie proposed that particles possess a wavelength related to their momentum:

\lambda = \frac{h}{p}

De Broglie wavelength, where h is Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)

This duality implies that particles are described not by definite positions and momenta, but by a wave function Ψ(r, t), which contains all possible information about the particle's state.

The Wave Function and Born Interpretation

The wave function Ψ(r, t) is generally a complex-valued function. Max Born proposed its probabilistic interpretation:

|\Psi(r, t)|^2 dV

Probability of finding the particle in an infinitesimal volume dV at position r and time t

The wave function must be normalized, meaning the total probability of finding the particle somewhere in space is 1:

\int |\Psi(r, t)|^2 dV = 1

Normalization condition for the wave function

The Schrödinger Equation

Erwin Schrödinger formulated the fundamental equation governing the time evolution of the wave function for a non-relativistic particle:

i\hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = \hat{H} \Psi

Time-dependent Schrödinger equation

Where ħ = h/2π is the reduced Planck constant, i is the imaginary unit, and Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator, representing the total energy of the system. For a particle of mass m in a potential V(r, t):

\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 + V(r, t)

Hamiltonian operator for a single particle

The Schrödinger equation is linear, meaning that if Ψ₁ and Ψ₂ are solutions, then any linear combination c₁Ψ₁ + c₂Ψ₂ is also a solution. This leads to the principle of superposition.

Formalism of Quantum Mechanics

The mathematical framework of quantum mechanics is built upon linear algebra in complex vector spaces, known as Hilbert spaces.

Hilbert Space and Dirac Notation

The state of a quantum system is represented by a state vector |ψ⟩ in a Hilbert space. Physical observables (like position, momentum, energy) are represented by Hermitian operators acting on these state vectors.

Dirac notation provides a convenient way to represent states and operations:

  • Ket vector |ψ⟩: Represents a state vector.
  • Bra vector ⟨φ|: Represents the Hermitian conjugate (dual) of a ket vector |φ⟩.
  • Inner product ⟨φ|ψ⟩: A complex number representing the projection of |ψ⟩ onto |φ⟩.
  • Outer product |ψ⟩⟨φ|: An operator.

Observables and Operators

To each physical observable A, there corresponds a Hermitian operator Â (Â† = Â). The possible results of measuring the observable A are the eigenvalues a of the operator Â:

\hat{A} |a\rangle = a |a\rangle

Eigenvalue equation for an observable Â

The eigenvectors |a⟩ corresponding to the eigenvalue a represent the states in which the observable A has the definite value a.

Measurement Postulate

When an observable A is measured on a system in state |ψ⟩, the result will be one of the eigenvalues a of Â. The probability of obtaining the eigenvalue a is given by:

P(a) = |\langle a | \psi \rangle|^2

Probability of measuring eigenvalue a

Immediately after the measurement yields the result a, the state of the system collapses to the corresponding eigenstate |a⟩.

Expectation Values

The expectation value (average value) of an observable A for a system in state |ψ⟩ is:

\langle A \rangle = \langle \psi | \hat{A} | \psi \rangle

Expectation value of observable A

Commutation Relations and Uncertainty Principle

The commutator of two operators Â and B̂ is defined as [Â, B̂] = ÂB̂ - B̂Â. If [Â, B̂] = 0, the operators commute, and the corresponding observables can be measured simultaneously with arbitrary precision.

If [Â, B̂] ≠ 0, the observables are incompatible. The generalized uncertainty principle quantifies the trade-off in the precision with which they can be known:

\sigma_A^2 \sigma_B^2 \ge \left( \frac{1}{2i} \langle [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \rangle \right)^2

Generalized uncertainty principle

For position (x̂) and momentum (p̂ₓ), the commutator is [x̂, p̂ₓ] = iħ, leading to the famous Heisenberg uncertainty principle:

\sigma_x \sigma_{p_x} \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}

Heisenberg uncertainty principle for position and momentum

Problem 1: Expectation Value of Energy

A particle is in a state |ψ⟩ = c₁|E₁⟩ + c₂|E₂⟩, where |E₁⟩ and |E₂⟩ are normalized energy eigenstates with eigenvalues E₁ and E₂ respectively. What is the expectation value of the energy?

Solution:

1. The expectation value of the Hamiltonian Ĥ is ⟨E⟩ = ⟨ψ|Ĥ|ψ⟩.

2. Substitute the state |ψ⟩:

⟨E⟩ = (c₁*⟨E₁| + c₂*⟨E₂|) Ĥ (c₁|E₁⟩ + c₂|E₂⟩)

3. Apply Ĥ to the kets: Ĥ|E₁⟩ = E₁|E₁⟩ and Ĥ|E₂⟩ = E₂|E₂⟩.

⟨E⟩ = (c₁*⟨E₁| + c₂*⟨E₂|) (c₁E₁|E₁⟩ + c₂E₂|E₂⟩)

4. Expand the expression:

⟨E⟩ = |c₁|²E₁⟨E₁|E₁⟩ + c₁*c₂E₂⟨E₁|E₂⟩ + c₂*c₁E₁⟨E₂|E₁⟩ + |c₂|²E₂⟨E₂|E₂⟩

5. Since the energy eigenstates are orthonormal, ⟨Eᵢ|Eⱼ⟩ = δᵢⱼ:

⟨E⟩ = |c₁|²E₁ + |c₂|²E₂

6. Note that |c₁|² and |c₂|² are the probabilities of measuring E₁ and E₂ respectively, and |c₁|² + |c₂|² = 1 for a normalized state.

Quantum Mechanics in One Dimension

Solving the time-independent Schrödinger equation for various one-dimensional potentials provides fundamental insights into quantum behavior.

Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

If the potential V(x) is independent of time, the wave function can be separated into spatial and temporal parts: Ψ(x, t) = ψ(x)e-iEt/ħ. The spatial part ψ(x) satisfies the time-independent Schrödinger equation:

-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx^2} + V(x) \psi(x) = E \psi(x)

Time-independent Schrödinger equation in 1D

This is an eigenvalue equation for the Hamiltonian operator, where E represents the allowed energy levels of the system.

Infinite Square Well

A particle confined to a region 0 < x < L with infinite potential barriers at the boundaries. The allowed energy levels are quantized:

E_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, ...

Energy levels for the infinite square well

The corresponding wave functions are standing waves:

\psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)

Wave functions for the infinite square well

Quantum Harmonic Oscillator

A particle subject to a potential V(x) = (1/2)mω²x². The energy levels are equally spaced:

E_n = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, ...

Energy levels for the quantum harmonic oscillator

The ground state (n=0) has a non-zero energy E₀ = (1/2)ħω, known as the zero-point energy, a consequence of the uncertainty principle.

The harmonic oscillator can be solved using analytical methods (Hermite polynomials) or more elegantly using ladder operators (creation and annihilation operators â† and â).

Quantum Tunneling

Unlike classical particles, quantum particles have a non-zero probability of penetrating potential barriers even if their energy E is less than the barrier height V₀. This phenomenon, known as quantum tunneling, is responsible for alpha decay, scanning tunneling microscopy, and certain processes in semiconductor devices.

Problem 2: Probability in Infinite Square Well

A particle is in the ground state (n=1) of an infinite square well of width L. What is the probability of finding the particle in the left half of the well (0 < x < L/2)?

Solution:

1. The ground state wave function is ψ₁(x) = √(2/L) sin(πx/L).

2. The probability density is |ψ₁(x)|² = (2/L) sin²(πx/L).

3. The probability of finding the particle in the region 0 < x < L/2 is:

P = ∫₀^(L/2) |ψ₁(x)|² dx = ∫₀^(L/2) (2/L) sin²(πx/L) dx

4. Use the identity sin²(θ) = (1 - cos(2θ))/2:

P = (2/L) ∫₀^(L/2) [1 - cos(2πx/L)]/2 dx

P = (1/L) ∫₀^(L/2) [1 - cos(2πx/L)] dx

5. Integrate:

P = (1/L) [x - (L/2π)sin(2πx/L)] |₀^(L/2)

P = (1/L) [(L/2 - (L/2π)sin(π)) - (0 - (L/2π)sin(0))]

P = (1/L) [L/2 - 0 - 0]

P = 1/2

6. The probability is exactly 1/2, as expected from the symmetry of the ground state wave function.

Quantum Mechanics in Three Dimensions and Angular Momentum

Extending quantum mechanics to three dimensions introduces the crucial concept of angular momentum and its quantization.

Schrödinger Equation in 3D

The time-independent Schrödinger equation in 3D is:

-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi(r) + V(r) \psi(r) = E \psi(r)

Time-independent Schrödinger equation in 3D

For central potentials V(r) = V(r), the equation is separable in spherical coordinates (r, θ, φ). The solutions involve spherical harmonics Ylm(θ, φ).

Angular Momentum

The orbital angular momentum operator L̂ is defined as L̂ = r̂ × p̂. Its components satisfy commutation relations:

[\hat{L}_x, \hat{L}_y] = i\hbar \hat{L}_z, \quad [\hat{L}_y, \hat{L}_z] = i\hbar \hat{L}_x, \quad [\hat{L}_z, \hat{L}_x] = i\hbar \hat{L}_y

Commutation relations for angular momentum components

The square of the total angular momentum L̂² commutes with each component: [L̂², L̂ᵢ] = 0.

The eigenvalues of L̂² and L̂z are quantized:

\hat{L}^2 |l, m\rangle = l(l+1)\hbar^2 |l, m\rangle

\hat{L}_z |l, m\rangle = m\hbar |l, m\rangle

Eigenvalues of L̂² and L̂z

Where l = 0, 1, 2, ... is the azimuthal quantum number, and m = -l, -l+1, ..., l-1, l is the magnetic quantum number.

The Hydrogen Atom

Solving the Schrödinger equation for the hydrogen atom (Coulomb potential V(r) = -e²/4πε₀r) yields quantized energy levels:

E_n = -\frac{m e^4}{2(4\pi\epsilon_0)^2 \hbar^2} \frac{1}{n^2} = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, ...

Energy levels of the hydrogen atom

Where n is the principal quantum number. The states are characterized by three quantum numbers (n, l, m), with constraints l < n and |m| ≤ l.

Spin and Identical Particles

Quantum mechanics reveals intrinsic properties of particles, such as spin, and imposes fundamental constraints on systems of identical particles.

Spin Angular Momentum

In addition to orbital angular momentum, elementary particles possess an intrinsic angular momentum called spin (Ŝ), which is purely quantum mechanical. Spin operators satisfy the same commutation relations as orbital angular momentum.

Electrons, protons, and neutrons are spin-1/2 particles, meaning the quantum number s = 1/2. The eigenvalues of Ŝ² and Ŝz are:

\hat{S}^2 |s, m_s\rangle = s(s+1)\hbar^2 |s, m_s\rangle = \frac{3}{4}\hbar^2 |s, m_s\rangle

\hat{S}_z |s, m_s\rangle = m_s\hbar |s, m_s\rangle, \quad m_s = \pm \frac{1}{2}

Eigenvalues of Ŝ² and Ŝz for a spin-1/2 particle

The Stern-Gerlach experiment provided direct evidence for the quantization of spin.

Addition of Angular Momenta

When combining angular momenta (e.g., orbital and spin, or spins of multiple particles), the total angular momentum Ĵ = L̂ + Ŝ is conserved. The possible values of the total angular momentum quantum number j range from |l-s| to l+s in integer steps.

Identical Particles and Pauli Exclusion Principle

Quantum mechanics treats identical particles (e.g., two electrons) as fundamentally indistinguishable. This leads to two classes of particles:

  • Bosons: Integer spin (e.g., photons, gluons, Higgs boson). The total wave function of a system of identical bosons must be symmetric under the exchange of any two particles.
  • Fermions: Half-integer spin (e.g., electrons, protons, neutrons, quarks). The total wave function of a system of identical fermions must be antisymmetric under the exchange of any two particles.

The antisymmetry requirement for fermions leads directly to the Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state simultaneously. This principle is crucial for understanding the structure of atoms (electron shells), the stability of matter, and the behavior of degenerate matter in stars.

Problem 3: Combining Two Spin-1/2 Particles

Consider a system of two spin-1/2 particles. What are the possible values for the total spin S and its z-component MS?

Solution:

1. Each particle has spin s₁ = 1/2 and s₂ = 1/2.

2. The possible values for the total spin quantum number S range from |s₁ - s₂| to s₁ + s₂ in integer steps.

S = |1/2 - 1/2| to 1/2 + 1/2 = 0 to 1.

3. So, the possible values for S are S = 0 and S = 1.

4. For S = 0 (singlet state), the possible values for the z-component MS are ms = -S, ..., +S. Thus, MS = 0.

5. For S = 1 (triplet state), the possible values for MS are MS = -1, 0, +1.

6. In total, there are 1 + 3 = 4 possible total spin states, which matches the 2 × 2 = 4 possible individual spin states (↑↑, ↑↓, ↓↑, ↓↓).

Approximation Methods

Exact solutions to the Schrödinger equation are only possible for a few simple systems. Approximation methods are essential for tackling more realistic problems.

Time-Independent Perturbation Theory

Used when the Hamiltonian Ĥ can be split into a solvable part Ĥ₀ and a small perturbation Ĥ': Ĥ = Ĥ₀ + Ĥ'. It provides corrections to the energy levels and wave functions of Ĥ₀.

To first order, the energy correction for a non-degenerate state |n⁰⟩ is:

E_n^1 = \langle n^0 | \hat{H}' | n^0 \rangle

First-order energy correction

The first-order correction to the wave function involves a sum over other unperturbed states.

Variational Principle

Provides an upper bound for the ground state energy Egs:

E_{gs} \le \frac{\langle \psi | \hat{H} | \psi \rangle}{\langle \psi | \psi \rangle}

Variational principle

By choosing a trial wave function |ψ⟩ with adjustable parameters and minimizing the expectation value of Ĥ, one can obtain an estimate for the ground state energy.

WKB Approximation

A semi-classical approximation valid when the potential V(x) varies slowly compared to the de Broglie wavelength. Useful for calculating tunneling probabilities and energy levels in slowly varying potentials.

Time-Dependent Perturbation Theory

Used to calculate transition rates between quantum states induced by a time-dependent perturbation Ĥ'(t). A key result is Fermi's Golden Rule, which gives the transition rate Γi→f from an initial state |i⟩ to a final state |f⟩:

\Gamma_{i \to f} = \frac{2\pi}{\hbar} |\langle f | \hat{H}' | i \rangle|^2 \rho(E_f)

Fermi's Golden Rule

Where |⟨f|Ĥ'|i⟩|² is the squared matrix element of the perturbation, and ρ(Ef) is the density of final states at energy Ef.

Problem 4: First-Order Perturbation of Infinite Well

Consider a particle in an infinite square well of width L. A small perturbing potential H' = V₀x/L is applied. Calculate the first-order correction to the ground state energy.

Solution:

1. The unperturbed ground state energy is E₁⁰ = π²ħ²/(2mL²).

2. The unperturbed ground state wave function is ψ₁⁰(x) = √(2/L) sin(πx/L).

3. The first-order energy correction is E₁¹ = ⟨ψ₁⁰|H'|ψ₁⁰⟩.

E₁¹ = ∫₀^L ψ₁⁰*(x) (V₀x/L) ψ₁⁰(x) dx

E₁¹ = ∫₀^L (2/L) sin²(πx/L) (V₀x/L) dx

E₁¹ = (2V₀/L²) ∫₀^L x sin²(πx/L) dx

4. Use sin²(θ) = (1 - cos(2θ))/2:

E₁¹ = (2V₀/L²) ∫₀^L x [1 - cos(2πx/L)]/2 dx

E₁¹ = (V₀/L²) ∫₀^L [x - x cos(2πx/L)] dx

5. Integrate:

∫₀^L x dx = [x²/2] |₀^L = L²/2

∫₀^L x cos(2πx/L) dx = [(L/2π)x sin(2πx/L) + (L²/4π²)cos(2πx/L)] |₀^L = 0

6. Substitute the results:

E₁¹ = (V₀/L²) [L²/2 - 0] = V₀/2

7. The first-order correction to the ground state energy is V₀/2.

Applications and Advanced Topics

Quantum mechanics has revolutionized our understanding of the universe and led to numerous technological advancements.

Atomic and Molecular Physics

Quantum mechanics explains the structure of atoms, the nature of chemical bonds, and the spectra of atoms and molecules, forming the foundation of modern chemistry.

Condensed Matter Physics

The behavior of electrons in solids, leading to phenomena like electrical conductivity, semiconductors, superconductivity, and magnetism, is governed by quantum mechanics.

Nuclear and Particle Physics

Quantum mechanics, extended to relativistic regimes (Quantum Field Theory), describes the structure of atomic nuclei, radioactive decay, and the fundamental particles and forces of nature.

Quantum Information and Computing

Exploiting quantum phenomena like superposition and entanglement opens possibilities for new computational paradigms (quantum computers) and secure communication methods (quantum cryptography).

Scattering Theory

Describes how particles interact and scatter off each other, crucial for interpreting experiments in particle physics and understanding reaction rates.

Relativistic Quantum Mechanics

Combining quantum mechanics with special relativity leads to the Dirac equation for spin-1/2 particles, predicting antimatter and providing a more complete description of electrons.

Interactive Visualization: Quantum Entanglement (Bell Test)

Entanglement is a purely quantum phenomenon where two or more particles become linked in such a way that they share the same fate, regardless of the distance separating them. Bell tests experimentally verify the non-local correlations predicted by quantum mechanics, ruling out local hidden variable theories.
