Radiative Transfer — Reference Module

Introduction

Radiative transfer is the study of the propagation of radiation through a medium, accounting for absorption, emission, and scattering processes. It forms the backbone of astrophysics, atmospheric physics, and optical physics, allowing us to understand how light interacts with matter across scales — from stellar interiors to interstellar clouds.

Core Topics

  • Radiation Intensity and Specific Intensity: Definition and physical meaning; dependence on direction, frequency, and time.
  • Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE): Differential form; source function; absorption and emission coefficients.
  • Optical Depth: Definition; meaning of optically thick and thin regimes.
  • Formal Solution of the RTE: Integrating factor method; boundary conditions.
  • Scattering Processes: Isotropic and anisotropic scattering; phase functions; albedo.
  • Local Thermodynamic Equilibrium (LTE): Assumptions and limitations.
  • Non-LTE Radiative Transfer: Basic concepts and challenges.
  • Line Formation and Profile: Absorption and emission lines; Doppler broadening; natural and collisional broadening.
  • Radiation Moments and Flux: Mean intensity, flux, radiation pressure.
  • Applications: Stellar atmospheres, molecular clouds, cosmic microwave background, accretion disks.

Recommended Textbooks

  • Radiative Processes in Astrophysics by George B. Rybicki & Alan P. Lightman — A canonical text, combining theory with astrophysical applications.
  • The Theory of Radiative Transfer by Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar — A classical and rigorous treatment.
  • Radiative Transfer by Robert J. Rutten — Concise notes, freely available online (see below).
  • Astrophysics of Gaseous Nebulae and Active Galactic Nuclei by Donald Osterbrock & Gary Ferland — Excellent coverage of emission line physics and radiative transfer in nebulae.
